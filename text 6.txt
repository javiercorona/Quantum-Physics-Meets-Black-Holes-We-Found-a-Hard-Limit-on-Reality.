In the Schwarzschild metric, the factor responsible for the coordinate singularity (where g^{rr} = 0) is \left(1 - \frac{2GM}{rc^2}\right).
In the Kerr metric, this factor is replaced by a more complex function that includes the angular momentum parameter, a. The determinant of the metric, \det(g_{\mu\nu}), which governs the singularity, is different.
1. No Singular Point, but a Singular Ring
The most crucial difference is how the physical singularity is handled:
 * Schwarzschild: The singularity is a point at r=0.
 * Kerr: The singularity is stretched into a ring at r=0.
This means a particle falling into a rotating black hole can miss the central singularity entirely by moving through the ring.

3. The Physical Interpretation: Stability
The Kerr solution is often viewed as being more "physically stable" than the Schwarzschild solution because it incorporates rotation.
 * By smoothing out the singularity from a point to a ring, and by defining the horizon with a more complex function, the Kerr metric provides a consistent mathematical framework where the maximum constrained momentum I_p would remain finite even at the horizon.
 * The constraint on I_p near the Kerr horizon would be extremely large, but it would not be infinite. It would still be set by the tiny values of \Delta x_{\text{min}} and \beta_0, but modulated by the large, yet finite, geometric factors of the spinning spacetime.
In essence, rotation acts as a regulator, preventing the quantum constraint on momentum from being completely broken by the geometric singularity of the static solution.
